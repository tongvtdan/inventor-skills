# The 40 TRIZ Inventive Principles

Developed by Genrich Altshuller from analysis of 400,000+ patents. Each principle is a generic innovation strategy — apply it to your specific problem context.

---

## How to Use This Reference

When the Contradiction Matrix recommends a principle number, look it up here. For each principle:
1. Read the definition
2. Read the examples from other domains
3. Ask: *"How can I apply this pattern to my specific problem?"*

---

## The 40 Principles

### 1. Segmentation
Divide an object or system into independent, separable parts. Increase the degree of fragmentation.
- *Examples*: Articulated truck (cab + trailer). Modular software architecture. Sectional sofa. SaaS multi-tenant vs. single-tenant.
- *Business*: Divide a monolithic process into specialized micro-teams or microservices.

### 2. Taking Out (Extraction)
Separate an interfering part or property from an object. Extract only what is needed, remove what causes problems.
- *Examples*: Move a noisy air compressor outside the building — pipes carry air in, noise stays out. Extract the sound from a music player (wireless headphones).
- *Business*: Extract the data layer from the UI layer. Outsource a function that creates internal friction.

### 3. Local Quality
Change from a uniform structure to a non-uniform one. Make each part function in a condition most suitable for its operation.
- *Examples*: Pencil with eraser (different materials for different ends). Graduated temperature in a furnace. A/B pricing tiers for different customer segments.
- *Business*: Personalized pricing, features, or UX per user segment instead of one-size-fits-all.

### 4. Asymmetry
Change from symmetrical to asymmetrical form. If already asymmetrical, increase the degree of asymmetry.
- *Examples*: Asymmetric tire tread for better wet/dry performance. Left-hand and right-hand scissors. Asymmetric negotiation strategies.
- *Business*: Asymmetric incentives (easy to start, harder to leave). Power asymmetry in marketplace dynamics.

### 5. Merging
Bring identical or similar objects together to perform parallel operations. Merge operations in time.
- *Examples*: Multi-blade razor. Combined heat and power (CHP) plant. Integrated circuit vs. discrete components.
- *Business*: Bundle features. Merge customer onboarding and first-value delivery into one step.

### 6. Universality
Make a part or object perform multiple functions, eliminating the need for other parts.
- *Examples*: Swiss Army knife. Smartphone (camera + phone + GPS + computer). A product manager who also does customer research.
- *Business*: A single feature that serves both acquisition and retention.

### 7. Nested Doll (Russian Dolls)
Place one object inside another. Pass one object through a cavity in another.
- *Examples*: Telescoping antenna. Retractable landing gear. Pull-out keyboard tray under a desk.
- *Business*: Embed a marketplace inside an existing app. Layer premium features inside a free tier.

### 8. Anti-Weight (Counterweight)
Compensate for the weight/undesirable effect of an object by merging it with another object that provides a lifting or opposing force.
- *Examples*: Crane counterweights. Floatation devices on underwater equipment. Carbon offsets.
- *Business*: Offset a poor UX step with a high-delight step immediately after.

### 9. Preliminary Anti-Action
Create beforehand stresses in an object that will oppose known undesirable working stresses.
- *Examples*: Pre-stressed concrete (compressed before load is applied). Tempered glass.
- *Business*: Churn prevention emails sent before a user goes inactive, not after.

### 10. Preliminary Action
Perform the required change to an object fully or partially before it is needed.
- *Examples*: Pre-loaded spring mechanism. Pre-packaged surgical kits. Lazy loading of UI components. Precomputed search results.
- *Business*: Pre-fill forms. Pre-build inventory ahead of peak season. Onboard users before they need a feature.

### 11. Beforehand Cushioning
Prepare emergency means in advance to compensate for relatively low reliability.
- *Examples*: Airbag in cars. Data backup systems. Redundant server architecture. Fallback UI states.
- *Business*: Feature flags for instant rollback. Failover logic. Graceful degradation of service.

### 12. Equipotentiality
Limit position changes by changing operating conditions so an object doesn't need to be raised or lowered.
- *Examples*: Car service pit (lower the floor instead of raising the car). Dock-level loading at warehouses.
- *Business*: Remove unnecessary state changes in a workflow. Flatten organizational hierarchy for faster decisions.

### 13. The Other Way Round (Inversion)
Invert the action used to solve the problem. Make fixed parts movable, movable parts fixed.
- *Examples*: Treadmill (person stationary, floor moves). Invert a mold to make casting. Escalator.
- *Business*: Instead of bringing customers to the product, bring the product to where customers already are. Reverse the sales funnel (product-led growth).

### 14. Spheroidality — Curvature
Use curvilinear or spherical forms instead of rectilinear. Go from flat to curved, from linear to rotary motion.
- *Examples*: Ball bearings. Spiral staircases. Curved displays. Rotary engine.
- *Business*: Circular economy model instead of linear. Recurring revenue instead of one-time sales.

### 15. Dynamics
Make object characteristics change to be optimal or find optimal operating conditions. Make rigid or fixed parts movable or adaptive.
- *Examples*: Adjustable car seat. Dynamic pricing. Variable wing sweep on aircraft. Adaptive algorithms.
- *Business*: Dynamic pricing, dynamic content, adaptive UI based on user behavior patterns.

### 16. Partial or Excessive Actions
If 100% is hard to achieve, use slightly less or slightly more. Then correct.
- *Examples*: Over-spray paint then remove excess. Overshoot target then back-off (servo control).
- *Business*: Over-deliver on first impression then calibrate. Send more invites than needed for a beta, then filter.

### 17. Another Dimension
Move into two-dimensional or three-dimensional space. Use multi-layered arrangements.
- *Examples*: Spiral conveyor belt. Multilevel parking. 3D printing. Layered architecture (OSI model).
- *Business*: Add a marketplace layer on top of a SaaS product. Create network effects by adding a social dimension.

### 18. Mechanical Vibration
Cause an object to oscillate or vibrate. Use resonance frequency. Use ultrasonic vibration combined with piezoelectric effects.
- *Examples*: Ultrasonic cleaning. Vibrating concrete compactor. Vibrating alert on phones.
- *Business*: Periodic "nudges" or notifications at resonant frequency for user behavior change. Pulse campaigns instead of constant marketing.

### 19. Periodic Action
Replace continuous action with periodic or pulsating actions. Change the frequency.
- *Examples*: Pulsed laser (more power per pulse than continuous). Intermittent windshield wipers.
- *Business*: Batch processing instead of real-time (when latency is acceptable). Sprint-based work instead of continuous flow.

### 20. Continuity of Useful Action
Carry on work continuously. Make all parts work at full load all the time. Eliminate idle or intermediate motions.
- *Examples*: Continuous casting in steel manufacturing. Async processing pipelines.
- *Business*: Automate manual handoffs. Eliminate waiting states in user flows. Always-on background processing.

### 21. Skipping (Rushing Through)
Conduct a process or certain stages at high speed to avoid harmful side effects.
- *Examples*: High-speed cutting to avoid material heating. Flash freezing food.
- *Business*: Fast track a pilot before stakeholder resistance builds. Launch an MVP before the market shifts.

### 22. Blessing in Disguise (Turn Lemons into Lemonade)
Use harmful factors, particularly harmful effects of the environment, to achieve a positive effect.
- *Examples*: Judo — use opponent's force against them. Regenerative braking converts braking energy to electricity.
- *Business*: Turn customer complaints into a feedback loop for innovation. Use churn data as product discovery.

### 23. Feedback
Introduce feedback to improve a process or action. Amplify existing feedback.
- *Examples*: Thermostat. Speed governor. User rating systems. A/B test feedback loops.
- *Business*: Embed feedback mechanisms directly in the product (NPS, in-app feedback). Real-time dashboards for sales teams.

### 24. Intermediary (Mediator)
Use an intermediary carrier, object, or process.
- *Examples*: Catalyst in a chemical reaction. Real estate agent. Payment gateway.
- *Business*: API as intermediary between platforms. Marketplace as intermediary between buyers and sellers.

### 25. Self-Service
Make an object serve itself by performing auxiliary helpful functions. Use waste resources or energy.
- *Examples*: Self-healing materials. Self-cleaning glass. Self-checkout in retail.
- *Business*: Self-service onboarding and documentation. Community-driven support forums.

### 26. Copying
Use simpler, inexpensive copies instead of unavailable, expensive, or fragile objects.
- *Examples*: Virtual reality training instead of real equipment. Crash test dummies instead of humans. Digital twin.
- *Business*: A/B test with prototypes before full build. Use mockups for user testing before dev.

### 27. Cheap Short-Living Objects
Replace an expensive, durable object with a collection of inexpensive ones, sacrificing longevity.
- *Examples*: Disposable surgical instruments. Single-use cameras. Throwaway prototypes.
- *Business*: Lean startup MVPs. Disposable landing pages for market testing. Temporary landing teams.

### 28. Mechanics Substitution
Replace a mechanical system with optical, acoustic, thermal, or olfactory signals.
- *Examples*: Optical fiber instead of copper wire. Ultrasound imaging. Smart sensor instead of physical gauge.
- *Business*: Replace manual reporting with automated dashboards. Replace physical sales visits with video demos.

### 29. Pneumatics and Hydraulics
Use gas or liquid parts of an object instead of solid parts.
- *Examples*: Air suspension. Hydraulic brakes. Waterjet cutting.
- *Business*: Fluid, adaptive pricing models instead of fixed tiers. Flexible org structures instead of rigid hierarchies.

### 30. Flexible Shells and Thin Films
Use flexible shells and thin films instead of three-dimensional structures.
- *Examples*: Bubble wrap. Gore-Tex membrane. Thin-film solar panels.
- *Business*: API wrappers (thin layer around existing infrastructure). Light abstraction layers rather than full rewrites.

### 31. Porous Materials
Make an object porous, or use supplementary porous elements. If an object is already porous, use the pores for a useful purpose.
- *Examples*: Oil-saturated porous bearings. Permeable pavement. Aerogel insulation.
- *Business*: Open APIs (permeable boundaries). Partner ecosystem that flows through your platform.

### 32. Color Changes
Change the color of an object or its environment. Change the transparency. Use colored tracers.
- *Examples*: UV-sensitive materials. Color-coded wiring. Warning lights.
- *Business*: Status indicators, progress bars, RAG dashboards. Heatmaps for UX analysis.

### 33. Homogeneity
Make objects interacting with a given object from the same material (or material with identical properties).
- *Examples*: Ice cutting board for ice sculpture. Biodegradable packaging for food.
- *Business*: Use the same stack for internal tools and customer-facing products. Build in the same language your customers use.

### 34. Discarding and Recovering
Make portions of an object that have fulfilled their functions disappear (dissolve, evaporate) or modify them directly during operation.
- *Examples*: Dissolvable surgical stitches. Sacrificial anode in marine coating.
- *Business*: Auto-expire free trials. Deprecate unused features automatically. Sunset technical debt on a schedule.

### 35. Parameter Changes (Phase, Concentration, Flexibility, Temperature)
Change an object's physical state (gas, liquid, solid). Change concentration or consistency. Change degree of flexibility.
- *Examples*: Shape memory alloys. Phase-change materials for thermal management. Viscosity-controlled fluids (magnetorheological).
- *Business*: Change the pricing structure (subscription ↔ usage-based). Change the business model rather than the product. Pivot the go-to-market motion.

### 36. Phase Transitions
Use phenomena occurring during phase transitions — volume changes, absorption or release of heat.
- *Examples*: Heat pumps. Steam power. Phase-change cooling.
- *Business*: Use inflection points in a market cycle (growth → maturity) to reposition.

### 37. Thermal Expansion
Use thermal expansion or contraction of materials. Use materials with different coefficients of thermal expansion.
- *Examples*: Bimetallic thermostat strips. Shrink-fit assembly (heat outer part to expand, cool inner part to contract).
- *Business*: Exploit market expansion phases — grow with the market, then contract to defend a niche as it matures.

### 38. Strong Oxidants (Enriched Atmosphere)
Replace common air with oxygen-enriched air, pure oxygen, or ionized oxygen.
- *Examples*: Oxygen-enhanced combustion. Ozone treatment for water purification.
- *Business*: Supercharge a process with a more potent resource — e.g., replace a human review step with ML pre-screening.

### 39. Inert Atmosphere
Replace normal environment with an inert one.
- *Examples*: Vacuum packaging. Nitrogen-filled tires. Argon atmosphere in welding.
- *Business*: Create a protected "sandbox" or isolated experiment environment to test risky changes.

### 40. Composite Materials
Transition from homogeneous to composite materials.
- *Examples*: Carbon fiber composite (strong + light). Reinforced concrete. Laminated glass.
- *Business*: Hybrid business models (SaaS + marketplace + services). Composite teams (internal + external talent).
